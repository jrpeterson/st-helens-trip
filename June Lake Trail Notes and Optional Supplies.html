01 / Conditions

Trail Notes

The Friday loop between June Lake and Ape Canyon climbs into the higher, exposed blast zone. Most of these are about terrain and timing rather than gear.

✅

Access is open

The south-side roads and trailheads are open for the season, and Ape Cave has been on timed-ticket entry since May 22, 2026.

❄

Possible lingering snow up high

High-elevation trails here typically melt out by mid-July, and snow can persist on north-facing slopes — exactly where the upper Loowit and Plains of Abraham run. Check the Swift Creek SNOTEL and the forecast the week of, and be ready for snow-covered stretches where the tread disappears.

⚠

Two washed-out gorge crossings — the crux

Between June Lake and Ape Canyon the Loowit crosses steep, washed-out ravines with loose rock that are difficult and potentially dangerous to scramble through. This is the most technical part of Friday: slow the pace, space people out, and spot the younger scouts. Trekking poles help a lot here.

🧭

Route-finding across the lava

Where the trail crosses lava flows and pumice, it's marked by posts and cairns and is easy to lose — parties report wandering off course. Have folks download offline maps (Gaia / AllTrails / CalTopo) in addition to the paper maps, compass, and GPS, and follow the posts.

💧

Water: top off at June Lake

June Lake is the reliable source. Beyond it expect very little — reports note only a trickle near the Ape Canyon junction, and snowmelt creeks are unreliable. Fill to full capacity at the lake before heading out (the addendum's 3 L youth / 4 L adult call is right for this).

☀

Exposed, sunny, and sometimes windy

Once in the blast zone there's no shade, and the Plains of Abraham can get windy. The early Friday start beats the heat and means crossing snowmelt creeks before afternoon flows peak. Sun protection and a wind layer (the rain shell works) earn their place.

🪶

It's dusty

The boulder and pumice sections are slow and very dusty. The bandana already on the packing list doubles as a dust mask; sunglasses help here too.

02 / Ape Cave

Ape Cave Notes

A few specifics for the Thursday cave stop.

Official requirement — two lights per person

The Gifford Pinchot National Forest requires each visitor to bring two sources of light to explore Ape Cave, and notes that a cell-phone light is not bright enough.

Source: U.S. Forest Service, Ape Cave Interpretive Site —
fs.usda.gov/r06/giffordpinchot/recreation/ape-cave-interpretive-site

🌡

Cold and pitch black

The cave holds a constant 42°F year-round and is completely dark, so an insulation layer (already on the list) and sturdy shoes are worth having on hand. Lanterns can be rented at Ape Headquarters (9 AM–5 PM) if needed.

🎟

Timed entry & cave rules

Arrive at the start of your reserved two-hour window. Inside the cave, food and drinks (except water), pets, and fires are not allowed. The Lower Cave is the easy family route (~1 hr); the Upper Cave adds an 8-ft rock-wall scramble and takes 2+ hrs.

🚗

Passes & parking

Ape Cave day-use is $5/vehicle (or a Northwest Forest / Interagency Pass) plus the timed reservation. The June Lake Trailhead and Marble Mountain Sno-Park are free in summer (the Sno-Park pass is winter-only). Worth confirming whether the Lava Canyon / Ape Canyon lot wants a Northwest Forest Pass.

03 / Gear

Optional Supply List

Not on the master checklist, but worth considering for this route. Tags: Recommended If snow Nice to have

  • Trekking poles — per personRecommended
    Big help across the washed-out ravines and the loose lava flows, plus any snow and the boulder scrambles.
  • Backup light + spare batteries — per personRecommended
    Meets the Forest Service two-lights-per-person rule for Ape Cave alongside the headlamp already on the list.
  • Microspikes / light tractionIf snow
    Only if the SNOTEL/forecast shows lingering snowfields on the upper Loowit or Plains of Abraham. Skip if it's melted out.
  • Blister kit (leukotape or moleskin)Nice to have
    A 10+ mile day on loose rock is hard on feet; cheap insurance beyond the basic first-aid kit.
  • Electrolyte tabs or drink mixNice to have
    Hot, exposed, sweaty day — pairs with the 1 L flavored-drink bottle already planned.
  • Whistle — per scoutNice to have
    Simple safety item for an exposed, split-group day; many packs already have one on the sternum strap.
